Foundry V13 Data Architecture: Exhaustive Sourcing and Integration of Zero-Cost Historical Crypto Trading Signals
The architectural development of the Foundry V13 crypto trading signal discovery system necessitates the ingestion, normalization, and quantitative analysis of high-fidelity historical market data. A critical constraint governing this phase of development is the requirement to source a minimum of 180 to 365 days of historical data for Open Interest (OI), Taker Buy/Sell Ratios, the US Dollar Index (DXY), Liquidation cascades, Decentralized Finance (DeFi) Utilization Rates, and Cross-Exchange Funding Rates at strictly zero cost. The system demands highly reliable data without reliance on paid Application Programming Interfaces (APIs), commercial vendor subscriptions, or temporary credit card trials.
Standard centralized exchange (CEX) REST APIs present immediate roadblocks. The Binance Futures API, for instance, imposes stringent temporal limits, restricting the historical retrieval of crucial market microstructure metrics—such as open interest and taker flow—to a rolling thirty-day window. Commercial data aggregators and analytics platforms construct their business models precisely around this scarcity. Platforms such as CoinGlass and Coinalyze gate profound historical depth behind premium subscription tiers, rendering their free or hobbyist APIs structurally inadequate for the rigorous backtesting protocols required by the Foundry V13 framework.
To bypass these institutional constraints and fulfill the required data prerequisites, a sophisticated data engineering matrix must be established. This matrix relies on leveraging public exchange data archives, community-maintained quantitative datasets, macroeconomic federal repositories, on-chain protocol aggregators, and the mathematical derivation of proxy signals from extant data. The following report provides an exhaustive, mathematically rigorous, and architecturally viable roadmap to secure the mandated data points. The analysis details the exact data schemas, API pagination strategies, proxy calculations, and systemic implementations required to reconstruct the missing datasets across the required assets: Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), Solana (SOL), Avalanche (AVAX), Dogecoin (DOGE), and Cardano (ADA).
Open Interest: Reconstructing the Core of Derivatives Liquidity
Open Interest represents the aggregate sum of all outstanding derivative contracts that have not been settled, expired, or closed. Within the context of the Foundry V13 signal discovery system, Open Interest is an indispensable, critical-priority metric utilized to determine market participation density, liquidity depth, and the probability of trend exhaustion. The foundational premise of tracking Open Interest dictates that a rising asset price accompanied by rising Open Interest indicates an influx of new capital driving the momentum, validating the trend. Conversely, a rising price coupled with declining Open Interest suggests that the upward trajectory is fueled by short-covering or short squeezes rather than organic demand, signaling an imminent reversal. Securing a minimum of 180 days, and ideally 365 days, of hourly or four-hour Open Interest data for the required USDT trading pairs is paramount.
The fundamental engineering problem arises from the Binance REST API endpoint /futures/data/openInterestHist, which actively truncates data retrieval to a mere 30-day lookback period. This necessitates an evaluation of third-party aggregators and alternative archival sources to fulfill the historical mandate.
An investigation into commercial aggregators reveals severe limitations for zero-cost backtesting architectures. Coinalyze operates predominantly on a freemium model; while it provides a robust suite of analytical dashboards and a free API, the historical depth available to non-paying users is heavily restricted. The free API tier is explicitly designed to serve real-time or near-term analysis, typically capping granular intraday data at a 30 to 60-day threshold to incentivize upgrades to premium tiers. Similarly, CoinGlass offers extensive endpoints for OHLC (Open, High, Low, Close) Open Interest history, but access is fiercely guarded. The CoinGlass entry-level "Hobbyist" tier, which costs $29 per month, does not even provide access to 1-minute, 5-minute, 15-minute, or 1-hour interval historical data. While the Hobbyist tier does provide 180 days of history for 4-hour intervals, it strictly violates the zero-cost mandate of the Foundry V13 project. Free, unregistered access to the CoinGlass API does not permit the automated extraction of deeply historical, granular Open Interest data required for algorithmic backtesting.
Alternative retail-focused platforms like Alternative.me are highly effective for extracting daily sentiment metrics, such as the Fear and Greed Index, which the Foundry V13 system already possesses, but they lack the microstructural focus required to serve derivatives data. CryptoCompare provides robust data feeds, but similar to CoinGlass, deep historical derivative data is cordoned off in professional or enterprise API packages. Dune Analytics, while a powerhouse for on-chain SQL querying, is structurally unsuited for centralized exchange derivatives data. Dune operates by indexing public blockchain ledgers; therefore, unless a community member has manually uploaded centralized exchange Open Interest CSVs to Dune's decentralized database, the platform cannot natively query Binance's internal derivative ledgers. BigQuery hosts public datasets for blockchain ledgers via Google Cloud, but querying massive datasets incurs computational costs, violating the strict free-tier requirement.
Consequently, the acquisition strategy must pivot toward primary data archives and community-curated repositories. The most reliable and historically profound source for Binance derivatives data is the Binance Public Data Vision repository. Binance actively maintains a publicly accessible, zero-cost archive of its market data, offering downloadable datasets for spot, USD-Margined (USDS-M) futures, and COIN-Margined futures. This repository stores comprehensive data dumps, mitigating the need for REST API pagination entirely.
Furthermore, the open-source developer community has actively bridged the gap between raw exchange archives and machine-learning-ready datasets. Repositories hosted on platforms like GitHub and Kaggle provide pre-compiled, sanitized Open Interest databases. A prominent example is the binance-futures-dataset-builder curated by Jesus Graterol, which specifically targets the extraction and formatting of Binance Open Interest, Funding Rates, and Taker Buy/Sell Volumes into unified CSV files. These datasets contain tens of thousands of historical entries, capturing Open Interest in both base asset terms (e.g., BTC) and quote asset value (USDT), stretching back well over the required 180-day minimum.

Taker Flow Analytics: Quantifying Aggressive Market Dynamics
The Taker Buy/Sell Ratio is a critical microstructural indicator utilized to dissect the aggression of market participants. In centralized limit order books, trades occur when an aggressive market order (the "taker") crosses the spread to execute against a passive limit order (the "maker"). The Taker Buy/Sell Ratio explicitly isolates these market orders, revealing whether aggressive buyers or aggressive sellers are dictating immediate price momentum. A ratio greater than 1.0 indicates that takers are buying more volume than they are selling, signaling bullish aggression. Because the Binance REST endpoint for this specific metric (/futures/data/takerlongshortRatio and /futures/data/takerBuySellVol) is restricted to the same 30-day historical boundary as Open Interest, alternative reconstruction methodologies are mandatory.
The commercial landscape presents the identical hurdles encountered during the Open Interest analysis. CoinGlass offers an endpoint for historical taker buy and sell volumes (/api/futures/aggregated-taker-buy-sell-volume/history), but this endpoint is subject to the stringent limitations of their premium-gated API tiers, preventing the automated extraction of deep historical arrays required by the Foundry V13 specifications.
However, a highly elegant, zero-cost proxy exists within standard historical Candlestick (Kline) data. Standard Kline data is universally accessible through the core Binance API and the Binance Data Vision archives dating back to the inception of the exchange, entirely unencumbered by the 30-day limitation applied to specialized derivative endpoints. Within the standard array of values returned by a Binance Kline response—Open, High, Low, Close, and Volume—the exchange also provides two critical, often-overlooked fields: Taker buy base asset volume and Taker buy quote asset volume.
The presence of the Taker buy base asset volume allows for the mathematically perfect reconstruction of the exact Taker Buy/Sell Ratio across any historical timeframe. Because the total Volume of a candlestick represents the absolute sum of all trades (both buys and sells), the taker sell volume can be isolated through simple subtraction.
The mathematical derivation is executed as follows: Let V_{total} represent the total aggregate volume within a specific 1-hour or 4-hour candlestick. Let V_{taker\_buy} represent the Taker buy base asset volume provided by the exchange for that same candlestick. Let V_{taker\_sell} represent the unknown taker sell volume.
Since every trade inherently involves a taker executing against a maker, the total volume executed is the sum of all taker buys and all taker sells: V_{total} = V_{taker\_buy} + V_{taker\_sell}
Therefore, the taker sell volume is derived linearly: V_{taker\_sell} = V_{total} - V_{taker\_buy}
Consequently, the absolute Taker Buy/Sell Ratio (R_{taker}) can be programmatically reconstructed: R_{taker} = \frac{V_{taker\_buy}}{V_{total} - V_{taker\_buy}}
By applying this exact calculation to the 1-hour and 4-hour historical Klines—data which the Foundry V13 system either already possesses or can freely download en masse—the system generates a flawlessly accurate Taker Buy/Sell Ratio spanning multiple years. This entirely circumvents the necessity to query restricted endpoints or rely on external aggregators.
The query regarding whether the volume delta between spot markets and futures markets can approximate taker flow requires nuanced microstructural analysis. While analyzing the divergence between spot volume and derivatives volume can indicate whether a rally is being driven by spot accumulation or leveraged speculation, it serves as a poor proxy for the directional taker flow itself. Spot volumes are heavily contaminated by algorithmic arbitrage, market-making wash trading, and basis trade hedging, which do not reflect directional, aggressive speculation. Therefore, relying on the mathematical derivation from the Taker buy base asset volume within the futures Klines remains the scientifically superior and unassailable methodology.

	Macroeconomic Regime Filtering: Harnessing the US Dollar Index (DXY)
Cryptocurrency asset classes, specifically Bitcoin and major Layer-1 smart contract protocols, exhibit profound sensitivity to global macroeconomic liquidity. Quantitative models continuously demonstrate a strong inverse correlation between the valuation of Bitcoin and the strength of the United States Dollar. When the US Dollar depreciates, global liquidity functionally expands, driving capital into risk-on assets such as cryptocurrencies and equities. The Foundry V13 system has identified a commanding -0.72 correlation coefficient between the DXY and Bitcoin, noting that a macro regime filter requiring a 2% drop in the DXY yields a 94% win rate for subsequent long algorithmic entries. Securing a minimum of one to two years of daily or 4-hour historical DXY data is therefore a high-priority mandate.
Scraping financial portals such as Yahoo Finance, TradingView, or Investing.com presents significant architectural vulnerabilities. These platforms deploy robust anti-scraping countermeasures, including Cloudflare captchas and dynamic DOM rendering, explicitly prohibiting automated data extraction in their Terms of Service (ToS). While Investing.com provides historical data tables, automating the extraction of this data via headless browsers violates institutional compliance standards and introduces severe instability into the data pipeline.
The premier, institutional-grade, and strictly ToS-compliant solution for macroeconomic data acquisition is the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) API, maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. The FRED repository offers immaculate, zero-cost access to thousands of economic time series, including comprehensive variations of the US Dollar index.
A critical quantitative distinction must be made regarding the exact index utilized for the regime filter. The standard ICE US Dollar Index (ticker: DXY) measures the US Dollar against a narrow, archaic basket of six major currencies, heavily weighted toward the Euro (approximately 57.6%). While the standard DXY is widely cited, it fails to capture the nuance of global capital flows, particularly those involving emerging markets where substantial cryptocurrency adoption and capital flight occur.
The FRED API offers a mathematically superior alternative: the Nominal Broad U.S. Dollar Index (Series ID: DTWEXBGS). This trade-weighted index evaluates the foreign exchange value of the US Dollar against a significantly broader basket of currencies, accounting for global trade dynamics and providing a more accurate representation of true fiat liquidity conditions. The DTWEXBGS series provides daily data updates extending back to January 2006, vastly exceeding the two-year historical requirement of the Foundry V13 architecture.

	Liquidation Data: Quantifying Cascading Market Failures
Liquidation data provides unparalleled insight into systemic forced unwinds. In cryptocurrency derivatives markets, high degrees of leverage result in cascading liquidations when price action sharply invalidates popular positioning. Monitoring liquidations allows a trading system to identify local extrema—moments of maximum pain where the forced selling (or buying) of over-leveraged actors completely exhausts directional momentum, setting the stage for aggressive mean-reversion trades. Obtaining 180+ days of hourly or 4-hour liquidation data for the six target assets presents a medium-priority challenge due to the ephemeral nature of the data streams.
The live Binance WebSocket stream (forceOrder) broadcasts liquidation events in real-time, but it is notoriously lossy. The stream only publishes the latest liquidation per symbol within each 1000-millisecond window, causing the system to blindly drop simultaneous liquidation events during periods of extreme volatility—the exact moments when the data is most critical. Coinalyze aggregates liquidation data, but their free API tier aggressively limits historical lookbacks, typically capping access at a 60-day threshold, falling drastically short of the 180-day requisite.
Fortunately, the open-source data science community actively rectifies this scarcity. Independent repositories, such as the crypto-market-data archive curated by Ercin Dedeoglu on GitHub, provide pre-aggregated daily liquidation datasets. These repositories host JSON files detailing BTC Long Liquidations, BTC Long Liquidations USD, and their short equivalents, stretching back over extended historical periods. While community repositories are incredibly valuable, they frequently focus exclusively on Bitcoin or Ethereum, potentially leaving a data vacuum for altcoins such as SOL, AVAX, DOGE, and ADA.
When explicit historical liquidation datasets for specific altcoins are unavailable, the Foundry V13 architecture must transition to utilizing synthetic liquidation proxies. Liquidations can be accurately inferred by triangulating extreme anomalies in Open Interest, Price Action, and Taker Volume.
The algorithmic logic for a Synthetic Long Liquidation Proxy is defined as follows:
1. Identify Open Interest Collapse: Calculate the first derivative (rate of change) of the Open Interest vector (\Delta OI_{t}). An abrupt, severe decline in Open Interest that exceeds three standard deviations (\sigma) from the rolling mean signifies mass contract destruction.
2. Correlate with Price Action: The Open Interest collapse must coincide with a sharp, negative price wick (a substantial decline in the asset's low price relative to the open).
3. Confirm with Taker Flow: The event must be accompanied by an extreme spike in Taker Sell Volume, indicating that long positions were forcibly converted into aggressive market sell orders by the exchange's risk engine.
Conversely, a Synthetic Short Liquidation Proxy requires identifying a simultaneous collapse in Open Interest, a violent upward price wick, and an extreme spike in Taker Buy Volume. By codifying this multivariate logic, the Foundry V13 system can retroactively scan its reconstructed Open Interest and Taker Volume databases (acquired in sections 1 and 2) to flag historical liquidation clusters across all assets, entirely bypassing the need for explicit, premium-gated liquidation data feeds.

Decentralized Finance (DeFi) Utilization: Measuring Systemic Leverage
The Utilization Rate within Decentralized Finance (DeFi) lending protocols functions as a profound macroeconomic indicator of systemic leverage and speculative appetite. Lending platforms such as Aave and Compound operate via algorithmically determined interest rates based on capital utilization. The Utilization Rate (U) is defined mathematically as the ratio of total borrowed assets to the total liquidity supplied to the protocol (U = \frac{Total\_Borrows}{Total\_Deposits}).
When protocol utilization exceeds the 90% threshold, borrowing costs spike exponentially. This state indicates that market participants are aggressively borrowing capital—often stablecoins to purchase volatile assets, or volatile assets to short-sell—signaling an overheated, hyper-leveraged ecosystem susceptible to cascading liquidations. Conversely, a utilization rate languishing below 30% indicates severe deleveraging, risk aversion, and a lack of speculative capital deployment. The Foundry V13 system correctly identifies this metric as a low-priority entry signal, but a high-value regime filter.
The challenge lies in sourcing 365+ days of daily historical utilization data for major protocols like Aave and Compound. Attempting to query the Ethereum blockchain directly via custom RPC nodes to reconstruct this history block-by-block is computationally expensive and architecturally prohibitive.
The premier solution for on-chain economic data aggregation is the DeFi Llama API. DeFi Llama operates as the industry standard for transparent, open-source DeFi analytics, maintaining a robust database of protocol metrics across hundreds of blockchains. Unlike centralized exchange data aggregators that aggressively monetize historical data, DeFi Llama provides extensive historical depth through its free public API.
While the DeFi Llama API does not feature an endpoint explicitly titled "utilization rate," it provides the raw components necessary for an instantaneous mathematical derivation. By querying the /protocol/{protocol} endpoint (e.g., /protocol/aave or /protocol/compound-v3), the API returns a comprehensive historical timeseries of the protocol's Total Value Locked (TVL), representing the deposited liquidity, alongside historical breakdowns of borrowed assets.
The Foundry V13 data pipeline can execute a daily batch query to the DeFi Llama API, extract the historical arrays for deposits and borrows, and compute the resulting utilization ratio vector. DeFi Llama's Python SDK (defillama-sdk) simplifies this extraction process immensely, allowing developers to pull protocol-specific histories utilizing a free-tier client configuration.
Dune Analytics serves as a powerful secondary resource. Dune allows users to execute complex SQL queries against parsed blockchain data, enabling the extraction of highly granular, asset-specific utilization rates (e.g., querying the exact utilization of the USDC pool on Aave V3 on the Ethereum mainnet). The platform's free tier allows for the execution of these queries and the public viewing of resulting dashboards. However, programmatically exporting these datasets via the Dune API consumes platform credits, and CSV downloads, while possible, are technically restricted to premium plans (Plus and Enterprise tiers). Therefore, while Dune is exceptional for manual verification and exploratory research, it is suboptimal for automated, zero-cost, programmatic data ingestion compared to the seamless REST architecture of DeFi Llama.

	Cross-Exchange Funding Rates: Arbitraging Sentiment Dispersions
Funding rates are the primary mechanism by which perpetual futures contracts remain tethered to the underlying spot index price. When the perpetual contract trades at a premium to the spot index, funding rates are positive, and long position holders pay short position holders. When the contract trades at a discount, funding rates turn negative, and shorts pay longs.
While absolute funding rates provide insight into aggregate market sentiment, the dispersion or spread of funding rates across different centralized exchanges represents a sophisticated alpha source. Because retail and institutional cohorts cluster on different exchanges—due to jurisdictional restrictions, fee tier incentives, or platform specificities—funding rates can diverge significantly. For example, if Binance exhibits deeply negative funding while Bybit and OKX maintain neutral funding, it indicates a localized, aggressive short-selling concentration on Binance. Exploiting these cross-sectional spreads requires the acquisition of 365+ days of 8-hour funding epoch data across Bybit, OKX, and Deribit to complement the data Foundry V13 already possesses for Binance and Hyperliquid.
Unlike Open Interest and Taker Flow data, which exchanges obscure or limit to monetize premium analytics services, historical funding rate data is universally provided by the native exchange APIs at zero cost. Exchanges broadcast this data freely to ensure transparency regarding the costs associated with holding leveraged positions.
Bybit Implementation: The Bybit V5 API provides a dedicated endpoint for historical funding rates: GET /v5/market/funding/history. This endpoint covers USDT, USDC, and Inverse perpetual contracts. The historical depth is functionally infinite, allowing systems to query back to the inception of the contract. The implementation complexity involves standard cursor pagination. The API accepts startTime and endTime parameters and returns a maximum of 200 records per request. The data pipeline must initiate a request at the current timestamp, extract the oldest timestamp from the returned array, and iteratively feed that timestamp back into the endTime parameter until the requisite 365 days of data are accumulated. The free-tier rate limits are exceedingly generous, permitting 30 to 50 requests per second for market data, ensuring rapid backfilling.
OKX Implementation: The OKX V5 API similarly exposes a zero-cost endpoint for deep historical retrieval: GET /api/v5/public/funding-rate-history. Like Bybit, this REST endpoint requires pagination. An architectural nuance critical to OKX integration is that while funding epochs typically occur every 8 hours, OKX dynamically alters settlement frequencies to 4-hour or 2-hour intervals during periods of extreme market volatility to force rapid price convergence. The ingestion script cannot blindly assume an 8-hour delta between records; it must strictly parse the fundingTime and nextFundingTime fields returned in the JSON payload to accurately align the time-series data with other exchanges. OKX rate limits for public data allow 3 requests per second per IP address, which is sufficient for historical extraction.
Deribit Implementation: Deribit, the premier institutional options and perpetuals venue, provides funding rate history via the public/get_funding_rate_history endpoint. Deribit calculates and applies funding on a continuous millisecond basis, but the API endpoint summarizes this data into 1-hour and 8-hour interest rate intervals for practical analysis. The endpoint requires instrument_name (e.g., "BTC-PERPETUAL"), start_timestamp, and end_timestamp parameters. Deribit's historical depth is vast, offering data back to May 2019 for the oldest contracts. The non-matching engine rate limit permits a sustained 20 requests per second, making it exceptionally fast to aggregate years of data.

	Strategic Priority Order and System Execution
To synthesize this exhaustive architectural review, the Foundry V13 system must sequence its engineering deployment to prioritize high-fidelity, low-complexity data acquisition while ensuring zero capital expenditure.
1. Proxy Signals from Extant Data (Immediate Execution): The highest priority is deriving signals mathematically from data the system already controls or can easily acquire without rate limits. The Taker Buy/Sell Ratio must be reconstructed immediately by extracting the Taker buy base asset volume from standard 1h/4h Binance Klines and subtracting it from total candlestick volume.
2. Free CSV/Bulk Downloads (High Priority): To bypass the 30-day API restriction on Open Interest, the system must ingest the pre-compiled CSV archives from the open-source Binance Futures Dataset Builder hosted on Kaggle/GitHub. This provides an instant, machine-learning-ready database covering 2+ years of Open Interest fluctuations. Similarly, community-curated GitHub repositories should be utilized to bulk-download historical Liquidation records for major assets.
3. Free REST APIs with 6+ Month History (Medium Priority): The system must deploy automated pagination scripts targeting the native REST APIs of Bybit, OKX, and Deribit to aggregate 365+ days of Cross-Exchange Funding Rates. Concurrently, API calls to the FRED API (DTWEXBGS) and the DeFi Llama API must be executed to construct the DXY macroeconomic filter and the Aave/Compound DeFi Utilization Rate regime filters, respectively.
4. Mathematical Proxy Modeling (Medium Priority): For altcoin liquidation data not captured by community datasets, the system must deploy algorithms to calculate Synthetic Liquidations by cross-referencing extreme negative standard deviations in Open Interest with price wicks and taker flow spikes.
5. Polling and Accumulation (Ongoing Maintenance): Moving forward, the architecture must abandon reliance on retroactive backfilling for ephemeral derivatives data. The system must instantiate permanent WebSocket listeners and cron-based REST polling daemons targeting Binance's /fapi/v1/openInterest and forceOrder endpoints. By self-hosting the continual accumulation of this data, Foundry V13 insulates itself from future exchange API restrictions and aggregator paywalls, establishing a proprietary, sovereign data moat.
6. Web Scraping / Advanced SQL (Last Resort): Scraping commercial platforms like Investing.com is explicitly rejected due to ToS violations and pipeline fragility. Query platforms like Dune Analytics are relegated to a secondary validation role, as programmatic CSV exports incur credit consumption contrary to the zero-cost mandate.
By executing this strategic framework, the Foundry V13 architecture secures a flawless, multi-year, multi-asset quantitative database, entirely circumventing the monetization barriers of modern cryptocurrency data syndicates.
